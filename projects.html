E-commerce Profitability Analysis & Machine Learning

Problem Statement:
In retail and e-commerce, high sales volume does not always translate into high profit. This project analyzes transaction-level data to identify the key drivers of profitability and evaluates machine learning models to understand and predict profit behavior.

Dataset:
Real-world e-commerce transaction data containing order details, product categories, discount information, regional data, and profit metrics.

Approach:

  • Performed data cleaning and preprocessing, including handling encoding issues and date features
  • Conducted exploratory data analysis to study sales, profit distribution, and discount impact
  • Identified loss-making transactions and analyzed their relationship with discounts and order size
  • Engineered business-driven features such as high-discount flags, shipping delay, and seasonality
  • Prepared data for modeling with proper encoding and train–test splitting

Machine Learning Models:

  • Linear Regression (baseline and interpretability)
  • Decision Tree Regressor (non-linear relationships)
  • Random Forest Regressor (ensemble model)

Evaluation Metrics:
RMSE, MAE, and R² were used to compare model performance and understand prediction limitations.

Key Insights:

  • High discounts (above ~40%) consistently resulted in negative profit
  • Sales volume alone did not guarantee profitability
  • Technology products generated the highest average profit
  • Furniture sub-categories such as tables contributed disproportionately to losses

Business Recommendations:

  • Limit aggressive discounting and apply discounts strategically
  • Focus marketing and inventory on high-margin product categories
  • Reassess pricing and sourcing strategies for consistently loss-making products
  • Use ML models as decision-support tools rather than automated profit predictors

Tools & Technologies:
Python, Pandas, NumPy, Scikit-learn, Matplotlib, Seaborn, Git, Jupyter Notebook

Outcome:
While profit proved difficult to predict precisely due to missing cost-related variables, the analysis delivered strong business insights and highlighted the importance of data quality and domain understanding over model complexity.
